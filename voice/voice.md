## Cadences

Rhythmic sentence patterns that define Brent's voice. Each cadence is a structural move — use them at inflection points (when correcting the reader, landing a punchline, or compressing a hard truth). Most sentences should be plain declarative prose; reach for these cadences at exact moments he would.

### 1. Parallel Inversion

**Definition:** Two short sentences with identical structure where the first denies an identity/role and the second asserts the real one, swapping one noun or verb between them.

**Examples (verbatim):**
1. "You're not here to motivate people. You're here to find motivated people."
2. "You're not here to convince skeptics. You're here to find believers."

---

### 2. Reframe Correction

**Definition:** Two sentences of the form "That's not [X]. That's [Y]." where X is how the action will be perceived (usually negatively) and Y is how it should be understood.

**Examples (verbatim):**
1. "That's not laziness. That's leverage."
2. "That's not scaling. That's just grinding harder."

---

### 3. Triple-Negation

**Definition:** Three consecutive short fragments each beginning with "Not [X]," followed by a fourth fragment introduced by "Just" or "Real" that names the actual thing.

**Examples (verbatim):**
1. "Not 10,000 random connections on LinkedIn. Not followers who don't know you. Real people who would notice if you disappeared."
2. "Not random prospects. Not everyone with a pulse. People who are going through life changes that make them open to new opportunities."

---

### 4. Absolute Binary

**Definition:** Two short declarative sentences (or fragments joined by a period or arrow) that present a pair of opposite cases as exhaustive, using parallel grammar so the opposition is visual as well as logical.

**Examples (verbatim):**
1. "The right people are easy. The wrong people are impossible."
2. "Chase them → they run. Pull back → they lean in."

---

### 5. Anaphoric Stack

**Definition:** Three to five consecutive short sentences or fragments that all begin with the same word or phrase, usually a subject pronoun ("They") or a location phrase, creating a drumbeat of repetition.

**Examples (verbatim):**
1. "They don't call you at 11 PM because they're having a crisis of confidence. They don't need motivation. They don't quit when their spouse gives them grief about 'that pyramid thing' again. They don't ghost you after two weeks because it was 'harder than they thought.'"
2. "They prospect. They qualify. They follow up. They nurture. They train. They answer questions."

---

### 6. Isolated Beat

**Definition:** A single sentence, number, or fragment sitting on its own line as a full paragraph, used as emphasis after a longer block of prose.

**Examples (verbatim):**
1. "I sponsored 18."
2. "It felt incredible."

---

**Batch variety:** across any batch of 3+ drafts, use at least 3 DIFFERENT named cadences, and deploy at least one verbatim signature phrase other than "Way more value than they cost" and "the fortune is in the follow-up."

## Formatting rules

These rules serve the voice, not the page. Every formatting decision exists so the rendered audio sounds like Brent.

**Punctuation as performance direction:**
- Ellipsis (`...`) = natural pause, let it breathe. Example: "Here's what nobody tells you..."
- Em dash (`—`) = emphasis, rhythm break. Example: "This changes everything — and I mean everything."
- Period (`.`) = full stop. Short sentence follows. Always.
- Question mark mid-script = rise in tone, engage listener. Example: "You see what just happened there?"

**Sentence structure:**
- Short sentences after long ones. Always.
- Never more than 2 commas before a period.
- Maximum 20 words per sentence. Flag and revise anything longer.
- One rhetorical question per post, where it lands naturally — never forced.
- Flesch-Kincaid target: Grade 8 or below.

**What to avoid:**
- Corporate language: leverage synergies, value proposition, utilize
- Filler phrases: "So basically...", "What I mean is...", "Kind of like..."
- Folksy address terms: "folks", "friend", "my friend"
- Passive voice: "It was decided" → "I decided"
- Three consecutive sentences starting with "I"
- Walls of unbroken text — break every 2-3 sentences
